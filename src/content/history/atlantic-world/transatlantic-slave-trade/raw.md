Between the 16th and 19th centuries, over 12 million Africans were forcibly transported across the Atlantic Ocean and into slavery in the Americas. It was unquestionably one of the darkest and most abhorrent chapters in human history. While the full horrors and scale of the transatlantic slave trade are wellnown, what's less understood is the system which enabled this tragedy to be carried out. an intricately engineered supply chain stretching over thousands of miles of ocean between Europe, Africa, and the Americas, which was designed at every stage to extract profit at the expense of human lives. But how did this system come about in the first place? How did it operate and evolve over the course of three centuries? And what was it that finally brought this injustice to an end? This is the history of the slave trade. Slavery is as old as human civilization. For as long as one group of people has been able to dominate another, they have found ways to put that dominance to use in the form of unfree labor. All of the empires, kingdoms, and states of the ancient world from Egypt, Greece, Rome, Persia, China, Africa, and even pre-Colombian America ran to some extent on enslaved labor. The buying and selling of human beings was woven into the economic fabric of the ancient world, and it remained so long after those empires fell. In early medieval Europe, the trade in human beings continued in various forms with Vikings taking captives from coastal raids and Frankish merchants doing brisk business in human cargo right across the continent. Gradually, however, the institution of slavery faded from Western Europe, being replaced instead by surfom under the feudal system across France, England, and the German territories by the dawn of the 14th century. Although the practice seemed to be dying out in the west, it was not dying elsewhere. The origins of what we now recognize as the transatlantic slave trade came from the area encompassing the Mediterranean basin, where the trade in people remained very much alive. This was structured along a fault line that would shape European attitudes towards slavery over the coming centuries, not being based on race, but religion. In the Middle Ages, the logic was simple. Christians did not enslave fellow Christians and Muslims did not enslave fellow Muslims. But both sides enslaved people of other faiths with very few qualms. Venetian and Genoise merchants were deeply embedded in this world, running a pair of sophisticated trading networks that included human cargo sourced from around the Balkans and Black Sea, with Bulgarians, Slavs, and Sakashians being sold in ports across the Mediterranean, both Christian and Muslim alike. However, these slave trade routes gradually began to be closed off over the course of the 14th and 15th centuries as the Ottoman Empire rose to prominence across the region. The Ottomans in turn forged similar slave trading routes of their own, redirecting captives from the Crimean Tartars who raided the Slavic step to markets in the Middle East. In North Africa too, the barbar states of Alers, Tunis, and Tripoli prayed on European shipping and coastal villages, not only in the Mediterranean, but also far out into the Atlantic. From Greece to Iceland, the Barbar pirates took as many as perhaps 1 million Europeans into servitude in North Africa between the 16th and 19th centuries. The slave trade, therefore, was not a European phenomenon. It was a global system with many buyers, many suppliers, and many trade routes, which had been in operation for many centuries. The rest of Africa was by no means exempt from this trade either. Practically all the civilizations that formed across the continent during the ancient, medieval, and early modern period were engaged in the slave trade. Arab middlemen transported millions of captives along the TransSaharan, Red Sea, and Indian Ocean trade routes to markets in the Middle East and beyond, long before any European ship ever reached the coast of Africa. What changed everything, however, was geography, technology, and ambition. In the early 15th century, Portugal began exploring southward along the Atlantic coast of Africa in the hopes of finding a direct sea route to the lucrative markets of Asia. The Portuguese, as well as their Spanish neighbors, were also complicit in enslaving non-Christian people across their lands. In past centuries, the majority of their slaves had been taken from among Iberia's Muslim population, as well as from passing Venetian and Genoies slave traders sailing from the Eastern Mediterranean. But as the multi-enturiesl long conflict known as the reconista gradually brought more of parts of Iberia under Christian rule and the expanding Ottoman Empire closed off the Balkan and Black Sea trade routes, both Portugal and Spain had fewer non-Christian people to enslave and so were forced to look at acquiring their slave labor elsewhere. In 1441, two Portuguese explorers, Nuno Tristau and Antonio Gonzaleves, reached as far as the coast of present-day Moritania and returned home with 12 captured Africans to be presented as gifts for Prince Henry the Navigator. It was an act of piracy more than commerce, but it nevertheless set a precedent. And by the 1460s, Portugal was taking 7 or 800 Africans a year, putting them to work as domestic servants or agricultural laborers in Lisbon, as well as on their Atlantic island plantations of Madiraa, the Azors, and the Cape Verde Islands, where sugarcane was already proving an enormously profitable plantation crop. Legal and moral legitimacy for these actions was granted by the papacy in Rome which gave a theological stamp of approval in 1452 with Pope Nicholas V issuing the bull dum diversus granting the Portuguese kings the right to enslave non-Christians in perpetuity. Two years later, Ramanasa's pontifffects extended this to cover newly discovered lands and their peoples. And in 1493, Pope Alexander V 6th issued intera dividing the non-Christian world between Spain and Portugal and endorsing the enslavement of Africans as well as indigenous Americans. The transformation of the early slave trade from smallcale coastal raiding into an industrial enterprise was driven by the European discovery and subsequent colonization of the Americas. After Christopher Columbus reached the new world in 1492, the Spanish and Portuguese promptly began carving out their own colonial empires across North and South America over the subsequent decades. However, both powers faced an immediate problem, a shortage of labor. Initially, the indigenous population had been forcibly put to work in building the foundations of the respective empires. But as more and more of the natives succumbed to the deadly and devastating effects of newly introduced European diseases to which they had no immunity, this supply of labor quickly died out. Plantation agriculture in the form of tobacco, cotton, coffee, and above all sugar required a vast constant workforce. European indentured servants were too few, too costly, and ultimately ill-prepared for working in the harsh tropical environments of the Americas. The Portuguese, therefore, turned to their already established networks operating along the West African coast. These were perfectly placed to supply the muchneeded slave labor in the expanding colonies. And so in the year 1526, the first recorded shipment of African slaves crossed the Atlantic to Brazil, thereby marking the beginning of one of the darkest chapters in human history, which would last for the next three centuries. The mechanisms that made the transatlantic slave trade possible were already embedded in African society. Slavery had existed across the continent for centuries as a consequence of warfare, debt, or judicial punishment. The Portuguese simply tapped into this pre-existing infrastructure rather than conducting their own slaving raids, which were not only costly, but also extremely dangerous. They instead established trading relationships with coastal chieftains and inland kingdoms who were willing to sell war captives in exchange for cloth, iron, firearms, and alcohol produced in Europe. By 1494, the Portuguese king had signed commercial agreements with several West African rulers, which allowed for fortified trading posts, otherwise known as factories, to be built along the coast over the following centuries, stretching from Senagle to Angola. It was from these stone enclosures that millions of enslaved Africans would pass through before being boarded onto ships that would transport them across the Atlantic. As this new lucrative trade route started to grow under the Portuguese and Spanish, soon other European powers began to muscle in. The Dutch emerged as a serious competitor in the early 17th century. They had already established colonies across the Indopacific region operating under the flag of the Dutch East India Company which was also heavily dependent on slave labor. However, it was the Dutch West India Company which began to displace the Portuguese from key West African forts and establish their own colonies across Brazil, the Caribbean, and North America. The French also followed suit, colonizing numerous Caribbean islands such as Martineique, Guadalupe, and Santa, as well as much of what is now Canada and the Midwestern United States in later years. By the mid7th century, the Atlantic slave trade had ceased to be an Iberian monopoly and had become a panuropean enterprise with all the major powers now involved. Realizing this, the Spanish began to outsource slave trading operations in their new world colonies to Portuguese, Dutch, and French traders in the form of the Asiento. However, it was the English and later British merchants who would take the slave trade to an entirely new level of scale and human suffering. Initially, English expeditions into the trade were tentative, being pioneered by individual merchant adventurers like Sir John Hawkins, who conducted several slave voyages during the mid6th century. But as England did not have any overseas colonies of its own during this time, and few foreign ports in the Americas were open to dealing with English slave traders, their involvement in the slave trade took something of a hiatus for the next h 100red years. It only resumed in the mid- 17th century after the colony of Virginia had been brought under crown authority and a formalized state sanctioned monopoly in the form of the company of royal adventurers trading to Africa was set up in 1660 permitting the trade and enslaved Africans. Although this was replaced in 1672 by the Royal African Company, an exclusive charter allowed only its merchants to sail from England and trade along the entire West African coast stretching from Morocco to the Cape of Good Hope. The profits from these voyages were considerable, especially in the early years of its operations, attracting investment from leading figures in England's financial establishment, including the Duke of York. It shipped more enslaved Africans to the Americas than any other single institution in the history of the slave trade, amounting to an estimated 186,000 people on over 600 voyages between the years 1672 and 1731. The monopoly, however, was always contested as private traders known as interlopers found the margins too attractive to resist and broke into the trade regardless of the exclusive charter. Plantation owners in the Caribbean even complained that the Royal African Company could not supply them fast enough. And so in 1698, the monopoly was broken and the trade was thrown open to all English merchants willing to pay a small fee. The effect was explosive. Within a generation, merchants in Bristol had overtaken their counterparts in London to become the leading slave trading port in the country by the 1730s. However, it was the port of Liverpool, further to the north, that was set to lead the way and dominate the trade for the remaining years of the 18th century. For much of its early history, Liverpool had been little more than a fishing village. But its growing involvement in the slave trade transformed it into a powerhouse of global commerce. The precise reasons for Liverpool's dominance were structural as well as commercial. Its hinterland was industrializing rapidly with the textile mills of Lanasher and the iron works of the Midlands producing cloth, metal tools, nails, firearms, and gunpowder. exactly the type of manufactured goods that African slave traders wanted in exchange for people. Liverpool also invested heavily into its dock infrastructure with the world's first commercial wet dock opening there in 1715, followed by six further dock investments by the end of the century. Ships built in the port would go on to account for 26% of all British slaving vessels. And by 1790, Liverpool would control around 80% of Britain's Atlantic slave trade and roughly 40% of all European slaving activity. A typical Liverpool slaving vessel would begin the first part of the voyage in what became known as the triangular trade after it had been outfitted and its cargo of goods to be sold in Africa had been loaded on board. It would then begin sailing due south, but in times of war, Liverpool ships could also sail north around Ireland, avoiding the sea lanes near mainland Europe, where enemy privateeers lurked, an advantage neither Bristol nor London enjoyed. Once the ship reached the African coast, it would anchor offshore or put into one of the great fortified trading posts. Liverpool's merchants had spent decades cultivating specific relationships with African rulers and traders, focusing their efforts particularly around the bite of Bafra and the kingdom of Bonnie, one of the most productive slaving areas on the entire coast. Captains who worked the trade regularly were known by name to the chiefs they dealt with, allowing for exclusive commercial relationships to form, which gave Liverpool merchants more favorable terms and better access to supply, which other traders could not compete with. Such was the closeness of these relationships that some African trading families even sent their sons to Liverpool to be educated, cementing ties that lasted for generations. Inside the fortified factories, hundreds of enslaved people were already being held, having been captured from the interior and marched to the coast over days or weeks. Once the exchange was ready to begin, the African traders brought their captives to the waterfront or to the fort's holding pens where the ship's captain and surgeons inspected them, haggling over prices in a currency of trade goods. The process could take weeks, sometimes months, as a ship accumulated the several hundred people needed to make the voyage economically viable. During this waiting period, the mortality rate among those already imprisoned was significant as disease moved quickly through the forts. Those who survived were then shackled in chains and loaded onto the ship. What followed was the crossing of the Atlantic, otherwise known as the middle passage, which could last anywhere from 6 to 8 weeks. Below the main deck, carpenters had pre-installed wooden shelf platforms, creating a double tier of sleeping surfaces. Each enslaved person was allocated a space barely larger than a coffin. Men were kept in irons, chained in pairs, whereas women and children had marginally more freedom of movement. The threat of revolt by the slaves on board was constant and real. Despite the chains, the guards, and the weaponry, rebellions occurred on hundreds of voyages throughout the history of the slave trade. Most were suppressed, but some briefly succeeded. Conditions on board were horrendous with diseases like dissentry, smallpox, and scurvy spreading with lethal speed and leading to mortality rates of around 15%. Not just for the slaves, but also amongst the crew. Although captains were financially incentivized to minimize deaths and deliver as many slaves as possible alive, the mathematics of overcrowding consistently worked against them. Those who survived the perilous middle passage, then arrived in the Caribbean or the American colonies depleted and disoriented after weeks in darkness and confinement. They were brought on deck to be washed before being presented at auction to plantation buyers. The slaves were then sold individually or in lots typically in exchange for quantities of sugar, rum, tobacco or cotton, the very slave grown produce of the plantation economy that had created the demand for their labor in the first place. A successful sale cleared the merchants accounts in the colony and the loaded ship prepared for its return leg to Britain. As the slave trade progressed throughout the 18th century though, dedicated cargo vessels known as West Indiamen were built to carry out this specific part of the route, transporting the plantation produce back to Britain faster and in greater quantity than any slave ship could return on its own. Once the ships arrived back in ports like Liverpool, the slave produced goods were then unloaded and either sold at immense profit or used as raw materials to produce the manufactured goods to be exported. The cycle of trade then repeated all over again. Underpinning all of this was a financial system of remarkable sophistication. Liverpool's slaving voyages were financed not by a single wealthy merchant but by syndicates of investors holding shares in the venture with the voyage being divided into 64 shares with as many as 1,400 individuals holding a stake across the fleet in any given year. This spread the risk across an enormous base of capital and drew money from far beyond the merchant class of Liverpool as manufacturers, lawyers, clergymen, and local politicians all purchased shares, resulting in the trade becoming deeply embedded within the town's economic and social fabric. When a voyage failed, as some did due to disease, storms, or rebellion, no single investor was ruined. And likewise, when a voyage succeeded, the profits were distributed proportionally. Liverpool's merchants also moved faster than their Bristol and London counterparts in settling accounts. Rather than waiting for cash to be sent from the colonies, they used bills of exchange drawn on London credit houses to settle transactions, turning capital over more quickly and reinvesting it into the next voyage while the previous one was still completing its circuit. In London itself, the coffee house that Edward Lloyd had established in 1688 had by the early 18th century evolved into the world's preeeminent marine insurance market. Every stage of the slaving voyage carried particular risk, and the underwriters at Lloyds were willing to price that risk accordingly and absorb it for a premium. Slaves were listed on policies as cargo and insured against loss in the same way as any other merchandise. The Bank of England also became involved by providing loans and extending lines of credit to finance the merchants operations, meaning that the wealth generated by the trade flowed across every aspect of the British economy. Despite the immense profits that could be made, a growing number of people across Europe, Africa, and the Americas had become conscious of the sheer brutality and immorality of enslaving their fellow human beings. The abolitionist movement in Britain at least had been born out of religious groups like the Quakers who condemned slavery overall as being uncchristian. The legality of slavery itself had been made clear in many European nations by this time where it was deemed that it did not exist under the rule of law as highlighted in the case of the slave James Somerset who escaped from his master while visiting Britain in 1771. But this ruling did not apply in other jurisdictions, whether they be African kingdoms where slaves were sourced or European overseas colonies where slaves were transported to and sold. As a result, slavery and the slave trade continued to exist and be practiced by Europeans through this legal loophole. However, one case that galvanized the British abolitionist movement more than any other came from events in late 1781. when the Liverpool slave ship Zong set sail from the coast of West Africa with 442 enslaved people on board, more than twice the number the vessel could safely carry. The voyage went badly. The captain became gravely ill and the ship overshot its destination in Jamaica after a navigational error, meaning that water supplies soon ran low. Then between the 29th of November and the 1st of December, the crew decided to throw 132 slaves into the sea, not to save their own lives or preserve water, but to ensure that the voyage would be financially successful. The ship's insurance stated that if the slaves died on board, then the owners would absorb the cost. Whereas if they were thrown overboard as cargo jettisoned to save the ship, the owners could claim insurance. The ship had been insured for $8,000 by a syndicate of Liverpool underwriters. But when the owners filed their claim, the insurers refused to pay. The resulting court case in 1783 came before Lord Chief Justice Mansfield who had also presided over the case of runaway slave James Somerset some 12 years earlier. He ruled that the insurers were not liable to cover the losses but crucially that no action would be taken against the crew for the killing of the slaves. Famously stating that the case was the same as if horses had been thrown overboard. The resulting public outrage from what became known as the Zong Massacre allowed the abolitionist movement to gather pace through the 1780s and 1790s. Enlightenment philosophers extended their arguments about the natural rights of man to include enslaved people. And a growing number of former slaves themselves like Oda Ecuano, whose 1789 autobiography became a bestseller, came forward to give the abolitionist case harrowing firsthand testaments of unimpeachable authority. William Wilberforce became the movement's parliamentary figurehead, introducing the first abolition bill in 1791, albeit unsuccessfully. He tried tirelessly to push the bill through parliament, but the slave trade and its wealth were so deeply embedded within the nation's political establishment that it proved difficult to gather support. The campaigners were nevertheless persistent, creative, and increasingly effective at mobilizing public opinion, leading to a boycott of slave grown sugar, attracting hundreds of thousands of participants. By the late 18th century, however, the trade had begun to look economically vulnerable. The plantation system was showing signs of strain. Sugar prices were falling and many Caribbean estates were heavily indebted. Some economists, most influentially Adam Smith, were arguing that free labor was more productive than enslaved labor, and that the slave trade distorted market values. The decisive factor came with the death of William Pit the Younger in 1806, a prime minister who, despite having personal abolitionist sympathies, had been reluctant to press the issue during his tenure. His successor William Grenville moved faster and in 1807 the slave trade act passed both houses of parliament prohibiting British ships from carrying enslaved people. Although it was not the abolition of slavery itself, which would come some 26 years later, it was the end of direct British involvement in the trade. The last British slaving vessel, the Kitty's Amelia, left Liverpool under Captain Hugh Crowe in July 1807. Ending the trade on paper was one thing, but enforcing it was another. Demand for enslaved labor in the Americas did not stop because Britain had outlawed its own participation. Portuguese and Spanish vessels continued trafficking Africans to Brazil and Cuba throughout the early 19th century. American slaveholders, whose Congress had officially banned the trade in 1808, continued to receive illegal shipments well into the 1850s. To address this, the Royal Navy established the West Africa Squadron, a permanent naval presence of the African coast, tasked with intercepting slave ships and liberating the humans on board. Between 1807 and 1867, the squadron freed around 150,000 people and seized or destroyed over 1,600 ships at the cost of spending tens of millions of pounds and losing thousands of its own sailors to fever and disease. The trade gradually slowed and eventually contracted, but it did not stop entirely with evidence suggesting that it continued in some form or another into the latter half of the century. The abolition of the slave trade and the abolition of slavery as an institution were separate struggles. In the British colonies, enslaved people had to wait until the slavery abolition act of 1833 to finally be set free. Extraordinarily, the British government decided to pay 20 million in compensation, equivalent of roughly 40% of the annual national budget, not to the formerly enslaved, but to the slaveholders for their loss of their human property. The compensation payments were so large that many descendants of slaveowning families were still benefiting from them in the 20th century with the government loan used to fund emancipation not being fully paid off until 2015. Although Britain led the way in ending slavery during the first half of the 19th century and had successfully persuaded many other nations to do the same, elsewhere, slavery unfortunately continued to linger. In the United States, it took a bitterly fought civil war and the introduction of the 13th amendment to end it. In Brazil, it persisted until 1888. On the Barbar coast of North Africa, the enslavement of predominantly Europeans only ended after numerous naval bombardments of cities like Alers and the conquest of the territory by France in the 1830s. The Ottoman Empire did not formally abolish slavery until 1908. And in parts of subsaharan Africa, various forms of unfree labor persisted well into the colonial era. The slave produced crops that underpinned the viability of the Atlantic trade continued to shape the global economy long after legal slavery ended. Cotton, sugar, and tobacco grown in the Americas flowed in everinccreasing quantities through the docklands of former slave trading ports like Liverpool, making them enormously wealthy over the remaining years of the 19th century. On the other hand, little changed for formerly enslaved people, although who were now nominally free, found themselves trapped in systems of debt and violence that replicated many of slavery's conditions which freedom alone had not eliminated. Overall, the Atlantic slave trade was one of the darkest and most abhorrent chapters in human history. It forcibly uprooted and transported somewhere between 12 and 13 million people across an ocean against their will. Perhaps 2 million or more died on the voyage, and many more died in the raids, forced marches, and in the holding forts before they ever saw a ship. It was at every stage a system designed and built to extract profit formed along an intricate supply chain engineered with the same attention to logistics, financing, insurance, and market intelligence that any merchant of the era would have applied to any other commodity. After all, the trade in human beings was nothing new and had continued more or less uninterrupted throughout human history up until the 19th century. being operated by seemingly ordinary people. Merchants, investors, lawyers, insurers, local chieftains, politicians, and plantation owners spread across Europe, Africa, and the Americas, who thought little wrong of a system that made the extraction of wealth from human lives not merely normal, but also legal. A fact that those cities, institutions, and economies that grew rich from it are still slowly coming to terms with to this day.
