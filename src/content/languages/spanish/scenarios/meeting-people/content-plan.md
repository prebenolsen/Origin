# Content plan - "Meeting People" (Phase 2, the social chapter)

*A proposed design and table of contents for the second Spanish goal,
`meeting-people`. This is the chapter where the language stops being a set of
counter transactions and starts being a conversation with another person. This is
a discussion + suggested ToC, not a full word list - each scenario shows only a
handful of example words/phrases/sentences to set the tone and scope.*

> Phase order: **1. Visiting Spain** (done) -> **2. Meeting People** (this) ->
> 3. Business Spanish -> 4. Living in Spain. Meeting People sits right after
> Visiting because it reuses that survival vocabulary and turns it social.

---

## Why this chapter is the one that "feels alive"

Visiting Spain is **one-directional and transactional**: you ask, you receive,
you leave. Nobody asks you anything back. Meeting People is the opposite - it is
**reciprocal**. The defining skill is not producing a sentence about yourself; it
is the loop: *say something, ask it back, react to the answer, keep it going.* The
single highest-value phrase in this entire chapter is two words long: **`y tu?`**
("and you?").

So the design shifts:

| | Visiting Spain | Meeting People |
|---|---|---|
| **Direction** | you -> the world (order, pay, leave) | you <-> a person (back and forth) |
| **Register** | polite stranger / `usted` | friendly `tu` - the informal default |
| **Goal of an exchange** | get the thing | build a little rapport |
| **Grammar weight** | fixed phrases | first real verbs you *conjugate* a little |
| **Emotional register** | competent tourist | someone people enjoy talking to |

**Carry over what worked in Visiting:** small batches of 3, lead with the most
useful word, personalize where the vocabulary is a matter of personal taste,
notes carry the culture, **keep `ñ`** (`español`, `compañero`, `años`) and strip
only the acute accents, one Spanish form per English meaning.

**Build on, don't repeat, `visiting-spain/introductions`.** That scenario is the
*one-way* intro (`me llamo`, `soy de`, `tengo ... años`). Meeting People makes the
same ground **reciprocal**: now you also *ask* (`como te llamas?`, `de donde
eres?`, `y tu?`) and *react* (`que bien!`, `yo tambien`). Reference it, assume it,
extend it.

### The new mechanics this chapter introduces

These should be **threaded through the scenarios as small notes, never taught as a
grammar lecture**:

- **`tu` is the default here.** Visiting Spain leaned on `usted` for officials and
  shopkeepers; among new acquaintances, peers and young people, Spaniards use `tu`.
  Say this once, clearly, in the first scenario.
- **A tiny, high-frequency verb kit.** Just the "you/I" forms, taught as phrases:
  `soy / eres` (ser - identity), `estoy / estas` (estar - state, "como estas?"),
  `tengo / tienes` (tener), `hago / haces` (hacer - "que haces?"), and the special
  one below.
- **`gustar` works backwards.** "I like X" is literally "X pleases me":
  `me gusta el futbol`. Plural likes take `gustan` (`me gustan los perros`). This
  is the one piece of structure worth its own short lesson - it unlocks every
  opinion in the chapter.
- **The reaction toolkit.** `y tu?`, `yo tambien` (me too), `a mi tambien`,
  `que bien!`, `de verdad?`, `que guay`. These are what make you sound like a
  person and not a phrasebook.

## Suggested table of contents

Ordered so each scenario hands the next one the words it needs. 8 scenarios is a
solid first cut; the last two can ship later as placeholders. Example Spanish
follows the project convention (ASCII, acute accents stripped, `ñ` kept, no
inverted `¿`/`¡`).

### 1. `introductions-social` - Meeting someone (the two-way intro)
*The reciprocal version of the Visiting intro: ask as well as tell, and close the
loop with "and you?".*
- `como te llamas?` - what's your name?
- `encantado` - pleased to meet you *(note: masculine form, said by a man)*
- `y tu?` - and you?
- *Example:* `Me llamo Preben. Y tu, como te llamas?`
- *Teaching note:* introduce `tu` as the friendly default here, vs the `usted` of
  Visiting Spain.

### 2. `where-youre-from` - Where you're from & where you live
*Nationality and home, both directions.*
- `de donde eres?` - where are you from?
- `soy de Noruega / soy noruego` - I'm from Norway / I'm Norwegian
- `vivo en...` - I live in...
- *Example:* `De donde eres? - Soy de Noruega, pero vivo en Madrid.`
- *Personalize candidate:* the learner's own nationality/adjective so the sentence
  they drill is true about them (reuses the onboarding `country`/`countryEs`).

### 3. `what-you-do` - Work & study
*"What do you do?" - the second question everyone asks.*
- `a que te dedicas?` - what do you do? (lit. "what do you dedicate yourself to")
- `soy profesor / trabajo en...` - I'm a teacher / I work at...
- `estudio...` - I study...
- *Example:* `A que te dedicas? - Soy ingeniero, trabajo en una empresa de software.`
- *Teaching note:* `soy` (identity) vs `trabajo en` (the place) - one short note.

### 4. `hobbies-freetime` - Hobbies & free time *(good for personalization)*
*Introduces `hacer` and "in my free time".*
- `en mi tiempo libre` - in my free time
- `me gusta + verb` - I like to... (`me gusta cocinar`)
- `hago deporte / toco la guitarra / juego al futbol`
- *Example:* `Que haces en tu tiempo libre? - Me gusta leer y hacer senderismo.`
- *Personalize:* "what do you do for fun?" -> only the learner's real hobbies
  (sports, music, cooking, gaming, hiking...), reusing the `personalize.json` pattern.

### 5. `likes-dislikes` - Saying what you like (the `gustar` centerpiece)
*The one scenario that earns a real mini-lesson on structure.*
- `me gusta / me gustan` - I like (sing. / plural)
- `me encanta` - I love it
- `no me gusta / odio` - I don't like / I hate
- `a mi tambien / a mi no` - me too / not me
- *Example:* `Me gusta el cafe pero no me gusta el te. - A mi tambien!`
- *Teaching note:* "X pleases me" - `gusta` for one thing, `gustan` for several.

### 6. `casual-questions` - Asking about someone
*The friendly follow-ups that keep a chat alive (their "casual questions").*
- `cuantos años tienes?` - how old are you? *(reused from Visiting, now two-way)*
- `tienes hermanos?` - do you have siblings?
- `cuanto tiempo llevas aqui?` - how long have you been here?
- *Example:* `Tienes hermanos? - Si, tengo una hermana. Y tu?`
- *Teaching note:* model the polite limits - what's normal small talk vs too
  personal on a first meeting.

### 7. `invitations` - Making & accepting plans
*From talking to actually doing something together.*
- `te apetece...?` / `quieres...?` - do you fancy...? / do you want to...?
- `quedamos?` - shall we meet up?
- `vale / me apetece / quiza otro dia` - okay / I'd love to / maybe another day
- *Example:* `Te apetece tomar algo? - Vale, quedamos a las ocho.`
- *Teaching note:* `tomar algo` ("have a drink/bite") is the default social
  invitation in Spain; `quedar` is the verb for "to meet up".

### 8. `compliments-smalltalk` - Compliments & easy conversation
*Warmth and the glue between topics - this is what makes you likeable.*
- `que bien hablas español!` - you speak Spanish so well!
- `me gusta tu...` - I like your...
- `que guapo / que guay / que bien` - how nice / cool / great
- `que tal? / como va todo?` - how's it going?
- *Example:* `Que bien hablas español! - Gracias, todavia estoy aprendiendo.`
- *Teaching note:* compliments are social currency in Spain; a genuine one opens
  doors. Keep them honest and non-romantic by default.

*(Optional later additions: `staying-in-touch` - swapping numbers/Instagram,
`hasta pronto`, `nos vemos`; and `at-a-party` - drinks, introductions in a group,
`este es mi amigo...`.)*

## Cross-cutting suggestions

- **Make `y tu?` a recurring beat.** Have several scenarios end their example with
  the question asked back. It trains the single most important social habit and
  ties the chapter together.
- **Thread the verb kit, don't lecture it.** Spread `soy/eres`, `tengo/tienes`,
  `hago/haces`, `estoy/estas` across scenarios 1-4 as item notes ("eres = you are";
  "haces = you do/make"). Give **only `gustar`** its own short structural lesson in
  scenario 5 - it's the one that pays for itself.
- **Personalize the taste-based scenarios:** `hobbies-freetime` and (optionally)
  `where-youre-from`. Nobody should drill `toco la guitarra` if they don't play.
- **Register note, once and clearly.** State the `tu` (informal, this chapter) vs
  `usted` (formal, Visiting Spain transactions) contrast in scenario 1 and let it
  ride; don't repeat it every scenario.
- **Reaction words punch above their weight.** A small recurring set - `yo
  tambien`, `que bien!`, `de verdad?`, `que guay` - in notes/examples does more for
  "sounding alive" than ten more nouns.
- **Honor the Visiting fixes from day one:** keep `ñ`; one Spanish form per English
  meaning; pronunciation hints in `note` for the tricky sounds this chapter adds -
  `ll` (`me llamo`, `ella`), `j`/`g` (`trabajo`, `guay`, `mujer`), `ñ` (`español`,
  `compañero`), and the Castilian `c`/`z` `th` (`hacer`, `dedicas`).

## Suggested first slice to actually build

The highest-value first three are **`introductions-social`, `likes-dislikes`,
`hobbies-freetime`** - they cover the two-way intro, the `gustar` engine that
powers every opinion, and a personalized hobbies scenario that immediately feels
relevant. Build those as `standard`/`personalized`, publish the rest as
`placeholder` scaffolds (English column filled, `es: ""`) so they show on the
roadmap without being shown to learners, then fill them over time.

When the goal goes live, remember the repo "required on every change" steps:

1. Add a `meeting-people` goal to `language.json` (positioned **after**
   `visiting-spain`, **before** `living-in-spain`), with its `scenarios` array, and
   set `available: true` once the first slice is authored.
2. Add each `<slug>` to that goal's `scenarios` array as it's built.
3. Bump the **MINOR** version in `version.js` + `package.json`.
4. Add a `docs/changelog.md` entry and mark the rows DONE in `docs/content.md`.
5. Validate: `node .claude/skills/spanish-content/scripts/validate-content.mjs`.
